City Life vs Countryside

/3

Put the words in brackets in the correct form/tense. There are two extra items.

Choosing where to live is one of the most significant decisions a family can make, with profound consequences for health, happiness, and quality of life. For many city dwellers, the appeal of rural life is driven by a desire for (clean)  of air, lower noise levels, and a stronger sense of genuine community belonging. Life in the countryside is undeniably (please)  in many respects: the pace is slower, the surroundings are greener, and human relationships tend to be warmer and more lasting. A housing survey found that families in major urban centres (crowd)  into increasingly smaller apartments as property prices rose steadily beyond what most households could afford. The countryside typically offers (consider)  more living space for the same financial outlay, making it an appealing choice for families with young children who need room to grow. Urban centres are becoming (crowd)  as unplanned population growth exceeds the capacity of existing infrastructure, creating chronic congestion and rising social tension. The (benefit)  of rural relocation — cleaner air, lower costs, and stronger community bonds — appear to outweigh the inconveniences for a growing number of former city residents. Whether the countryside or the city offers a better quality of life ultimately depends on the individual needs, priorities, and circumstances of each family.

📚 Grammar Rules to Revise
  • -ness nouns: clean→cleanliness, kind→kindness, happy→happiness.
  • -ant adjectives: please→pleasant, dominate→dominant, signify→significant.
  • Past continuous passive: was/were being + past participle.

Migration, Community & Quality of Life

/3

Put the words in brackets in the correct form/tense. There are two extra items.

The movement of people between cities and the countryside reflects deeper changes in the way societies understand prosperity and quality of life. The (migrate)  of young families from cities to smaller towns has accelerated sharply in recent years, driven by rising urban costs and a desire for a better life. Many former city residents describe feeling deeply (revitalise)  by their new surroundings — particularly by the cleaner air, the quieter streets, and the greater sense of personal space. In recent decades, the rural economy in many parts of Tunisia (grow)  steadily as former urban professionals have brought skills, investment, and energy to smaller communities. The government recommends (invest)  in rural broadband infrastructure as a priority, arguing that fast internet is now as essential as roads and electricity to economic development. Communities that have welcomed new residents report feeling less (connect)  from the rest of society and more confident about their long-term social and economic prospects. The (compare)  between rural and urban quality of life is never simple — both environments offer distinct advantages that vary greatly according to individual needs and priorities. What matters most, in the end, is not where people live but whether their environment supports their health, their relationships, and their sense of purpose.

📚 Grammar Rules to Revise
  • -tion nouns: migrate→migration, educate→education (drop -e + -tion).
  • -ed adjective (state): feel revitalised/motivated/exhausted — past participle after feel/seem.
  • Present perfect: has/have + past participle. Use with: in recent years/decades.
  • Gerund after recommend: recommend + verb-ing. Also: suggest/avoid/enjoy + verb-ing.
  • Prefix dis- + -ed: connected→disconnected, satisfied→dissatisfied.
  • Irregular noun -ison: compare→comparison, divide→division.
